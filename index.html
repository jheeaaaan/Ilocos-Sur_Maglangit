Background

History of Ilocos Sur

Ilocos Sur is one of the oldest provinces in the Philippines. Long before the Spanish arrived, the region was already home to the Ilocano people, a resilient and industrious group who built communities along the coast and mountains of northwestern Luzon.

The Spanish colonizers arrived in 1572 under Juan de Salcedo, and the province was officially established during this period. The Spanish brought their religion, architecture, and governance, which deeply shaped the culture of Ilocos Sur. Baroque churches, colonial mansions, and cobblestone streets were built across the province — many of which still stand today.

Vigan City, the provincial capital, became one of the most important trading centers in Asia during the Spanish era. Its strategic location made it a melting pot of Filipino, Spanish, Chinese, and Japanese culture, which is why Vigan's architecture looks unlike any other city in Southeast Asia.

In 1999, UNESCO declared the Historic City of Vigan a World Heritage Site, recognizing it as one of the best-preserved examples of a planned Spanish colonial town in Asia. Today, Ilocos Sur continues to honor its past while embracing the future.

Native Tongue

The Ilocano Language


The people of Ilocos Sur speak Ilocano (also spelled "Ilokano"), which is the third most spoken language in the Philippines after Filipino and Cebuano. It belongs to the Austronesian language family and has been spoken in the Ilocos region for thousands of years.

Ilocano is known for its distinct, sharp sounds and straightforward grammar. Unlike Tagalog, many Ilocano words come from older Austronesian roots. The language even has its own traditional script called Kur-itan, though it is rarely used today.

Most Ilocanos also speak Filipino (Tagalog) and English, especially in schools and government. But in markets, homes, and everyday conversation, Ilocano is still the language of the people.

The word "Ilocano" comes from i- meaning "people of" and looc meaning "bay" so Ilocano literally means "people of the bay." This reflects the deep coastal roots of the province and its culture.
