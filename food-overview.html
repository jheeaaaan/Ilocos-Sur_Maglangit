Ilocano Food Tourism

If there is one thing Ilocanos are fiercely proud of — aside from their heritage, it is their food. Ilocano cuisine is bold, salty, deeply savory, and unlike anything else in the Philippines. It developed out of necessity in a region with harsh winds, scarce rain, and land that was not always easy to farm. Ilocanos learned to preserve, ferment, and intensify flavor in ways that turned simple ingredients into some of the most beloved dishes in the country.

The Ilocano Kitchen

What Makes Ilocano Cuisine Unique

Ilocano cuisine is built around a few key principles: bagoong (fermented shrimp or fish paste), vinegar, and the art of deep-frying until something becomes impossibly crispy. These were not just flavor preferences they were survival techniques. Fermentation preserved food without refrigeration, and frying extended the shelf life of meat in the hot Ilocos climate.

The result is a cuisine that hits hard on flavor. The longganisa here is garlicky and slightly sour, nothing like the sweet versions elsewhere. The bagnet is pork belly fried twice until it shatters like glass. Even the vegetables in pinakbet are cooked with bitter, salty bagoong that makes the whole dish taste like the earth itself.

Ilocano food is also famously budget-friendly. A full meal at a local carinderia in Vigan rice, viand, soup can cost as little as ₱80 to ₱120. Even the fanciest restaurant in the heritage zone will not break the bank. Eating well in Ilocos Sur is easy and cheap, which is one more reason to visit.

Bagoong
Fermented shrimp or fish paste the backbone of Ilocano cooking. Used in pinakbet, dinengdeng, and as a dipping sauce for bagnet.
Garlic
Ilocos Sur is one of the top garlic-producing regions in the Philippines. Ilocano garlic is smaller, more pungent, and intensely flavored.
Sukang Iloco
Ilocano sugarcane vinegar used in cooking and as a condiment. Milder and slightly sweeter than regular vinegar, with a distinct aroma.
Burnay Jars
Traditional clay pots made from Ilocos Sur's red soil, used for centuries to ferment and store bagoong, vinegar, and other preserved foods.
🧄 Fun Fact — Ilocos Garlic is Different

Ilocos Sur is the garlic capital of the Philippines. The garlic grown here in the dry, mineral-rich Ilocos soil is smaller than commercial garlic but far more pungent and aromatic. Ilocano garlic is so prized that it is sold all over the country and even exported. This is exactly why Vigan Longganisa tastes so intensely garlicky it uses locally grown Ilocos garlic, not the mild imported kind. You can buy fresh Ilocos garlic braids at the Vigan Public Market as a souvenir.

Fermented & Preserved

The Culture of Bagoong

No discussion of Ilocano food is complete without talking about bagoong. This fermented shrimp or fish paste is used in nearly every traditional Ilocano dish. It serves as a seasoning, a condiment, and sometimes the main flavor of an entire meal. Ilocanos put it on vegetables, dip their bagnet in it, and add it to soups and stews.

The bagoong of Ilocos Sur is particularly famous because it is traditionally fermented inside burnay jars the handmade clay pots from Vigan. The clay is porous enough to let the fermentation breathe but sealed enough to concentrate the flavor over months or even years. The longer it ferments, the deeper and richer the flavor becomes.

Aramang
The most common type of Ilocano bagoong made from tiny pink shrimp called aramang. Bright pink in color, salty, and deeply savory.
Bugguong
Fermented fish paste stronger and darker than aramang. Used mainly in dinengdeng to add a deep, funky umami flavor to the broth.
In Burnay
Traditionally fermented inside burnay clay jars for months. The porous clay allows slow fermentation that develops complex flavors over time.
As Pasalubong
Vacuum-packed bagoong is one of the most popular pasalubong items from Ilocos Sur. Available at the Vigan Public Market and souvenir shops.
🍤 Fun Fact — Bagoong & the Burnay Connection

The Pagburnayan area in Vigan the street where burnay jar makers have been working for centuries exists largely because of bagoong. Fermented pastes and sauces needed to be stored in clay containers that could breathe without leaking, and the red clay soil of Ilocos Sur turned out to be perfect for making exactly that kind of jar. So in a way, the entire pottery tradition of Vigan exists because of the Ilocano love for fermented food. 🏺
